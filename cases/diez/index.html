Diez — a chord app for guitar players.

Context

Why I’m building this.

I play guitar. The tools I use to learn songs and build chord progressions are either heavy desktop software or ad-soaked mobile sites. There’s a gap for a quiet, focused, mobile-first chord app — and I’m close enough to the user that I can make decisions without a research panel.

Beyond the product itself, Diez is my way of practising what I preach: a designer can — and should — own a full product loop at least once. Strategy, research, design, build, ship, monetise, learn.

Approach

Indie product thinking, not “app design”.

  1. Unit economics firstModelled on the framework I learned at Projector’s Unit Economics course — CAC, LTV, ARPU, retention cohorts, monetisation paths.
  2. Mobile-first by defaultA guitar player reaches for their phone, not a laptop, when they need a chord.
  3. Tiny, opinionated scopeAn MVP that does one thing — chord library + simple progression builder — and does it well.
  4. Design ↔ engineering as one loopI’m both. No hand-offs. Decisions in Figma get tested in the browser the same day.
What I’m shipping

An MVP, then real-world signal.

  • Chord libraryBrowse, search, and bookmark chords with clear fingering diagrams.
  • Progression builderDrag chords into a sequence, save it, share it.
  • Mobile-first interfaceOne-handed use, comfortable while holding a guitar.
  • Monetisation hypothesisModelled but unproven — a subscription paywall on richer features, with a generous free tier.
Outcome (so far)

It’s real, it’s coming, and I’m learning.

IndieProduct model

The headline outcome is honest: it’s still pre-launch. What it gives me — and what it would give a team that hires me — is an inside view of indie product economics, monetisation trade-offs and the cost of every “small” design decision when you’re the one paying the engineering bill.

Learnings so far

What building Diez is teaching me.

  • Scope is the designEvery screen costs me engineering time. Cutting is design, not laziness.
  • Mobile-first is a discipline, not a buzzwordDesigning for one-handed phone use forces clarity. The desktop version usually inherits it well.
  • Monetisation is part of UXWhere the paywall sits is a design decision with retention consequences.
